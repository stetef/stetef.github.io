  • Visions of the Dead

    This short story was written as the beginning of a longer story. It is a retelling of Persephone, but with steampunk elements.

    I crouch down into the brush, bringing the camouflaged hood of my cloak above my head. The earth underneath my knees is soft. The scent of decaying leaves wafts up from the forest floor, the crisp air quickly cooling the sweat that has collected on my brow. I wrap a scarf around my nose and mouth so that my condensing breath does not give me away.

    An orb spider has decided to spin a web in the exact clearing I made that very morning, its web spanning the small opening in the leaves that acts as my vantage point.

    When did you even have the time?

    I take a fallen leaf, mostly holes now courtesy of some insect’s appetite, and spindle it around the web. I toss the dead leaf, now covered in spider silk, behind my shoulder. The spider, however, is on the ground where it fell, perfectly still. I try to scurry it along, waving my fingers behind its butt, but it doesn’t budge.

    Move.

    It refuses.

    I sigh and scoop the stunned thing up into my hands, noticing that pyrofer sap still coats my fingers. I lay the spider down underneath the thorns of a blackberry bush located a few feet behind me, outside the blast zone. The fruit has long since fallen and decayed.

    I return to my chosen spot and wipe my hands on my thighs. Nothing happens, other than collecting a thin layer dust from my leather pants. So I wipe them on the carpet of leaves at my knees. This time, the sticky residue adheres to goblets of mud and disintegrating plant matter, collecting in wads between my fingers. I rub my hands on my pants again, fingers splayed to smear off as much of the muddy goop as possible. My hands are no longer sticky, even if a layer of dirt is now glued onto them.

    Whatever. Good enough. My hands won’t combust.

    I slip my archer tab onto my right hand and flex it a few times, reveling in the feel of the soft leather. I unstrap the longbow from my back and peer through the break in the leaves of my hiding spot.

    I settle in, resting my bones into a position I know I can hold for hours, boots planted firmly in the frozen ground. I focus on my breathing, my slowing heartrate. I wait.

    The only way up the mountain to the pass above is via this trail, the other paths of ascent blocked by steep cliffs or raging rivers during this time of the year.

    Clouds start to blow across the valley, but their slow movements indicate I still have a few hours before the rain sets in.

    Then, the murmur of voices and the clop clop clop of horse hooves nears. As the hunting party rounds the bend, entering the straight away that approaches my hiding spot, I finally get a good look at my prey. There are five of them, all on horses and clad in silver-plated armor. A laden donkey brings up the rear. The man in the back is the only one with a ranged weapon, a bow attached to the saddle for quick access. The rest have swords.

    I breathe deeply through my scarf, trying to lower my spiking heartrate. I planned for that, but I’ll need to keep an eye on the archer.
